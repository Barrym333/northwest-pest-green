Ants


In the UK, there are several species of ants, with some being more common than others. Heres an overview of the most notable ones:

Black Garden Ant (Lasius niger)

: The most common ant in the UK, often found in gardens and homes.

: Typically black or dark brown and around 3–5mm in length.

: Lives in large colonies, often nesting under patios, pavements, or in garden soil.

: Attracted to sweet foods and can become a household nuisance.


Red Ant (Myrmica rubra)

: Also known as the European fire ant, these ants are reddish-brown.

: Can deliver a mild sting when disturbed.

: Nests are usually found in soil, under rocks, or in decaying wood.


Wood Ant (Formica rufa)

: Larger than common garden ants, reaching up to 10mm in length.

: Typically reddish-brown with a dark abdomen.

: Builds large, visible mounds in forests made from twigs, leaves, and pine needles.

: Plays an important ecological role in woodland environments.


Pharaoh Ant (Monomorium pharaonis)

: A small, yellow-brown species that prefers warm, indoor environments.

: Often found in hospitals, kitchens, and heated buildings.

: Difficult to control due to their ability to form multiple colonies.


Ghost Ant (Tapinoma melanocephalum)

: Very small, with a dark head and pale, almost translucent body.

: Prefers warm and humid environments, often nesting in walls or behind kitchen appliances.

: Attracted to sweet and greasy foods.


Ant Behaviour in the UK

: Most ants are social insects, living in colonies with a queen, workers, and, at certain times, flying reproductive ants.

: Flying ants" appear during summer (usually in July or August) when reproductive males and queens swarm to mate.

: They primarily feed on sugary substances, plant nectar, dead insects, and food scraps.


Problems Caused by Ants

: While most UK ants are harmless, they can become a nuisance when they invade homes.

: Some species, like Pharaoh ants, can spread bacteria in kitchens and healthcare settings.

: Wood ants can bite, but their bites are not harmful to humans.


Bedbugs in the UK (Cimex lectularius)


Bedbugs are small, reddish-brown, parasitic insects that feed on human blood. In the UK, bedbug infestations have become more common in recent years, particularly in densely populated areas.

: Appearance & Characteristics, Flat, oval-shaped bodies (before feeding).

: Around 4-6mm in length (about the size of an apple seed).

: Reddish-brown in colour, turning darker after feeding.

: Six legs and short, non-functional wings (they cannot fly).

: Move quickly but do not jump.

Where Are They Found?

: Commonly hide in mattresses, bed frames, headboards, carpets, skirting boards, and furniture crevices.

: Can also be found in hotels, public transport, cinemas, and offices.

: Spread through luggage, second-hand furniture, and clothing.

Signs of Bedbug Infestation:

: Bite Marks, Red, itchy bites often appearing in clusters or straight lines, usually on exposed skin (arms, neck, face, and legs).

: Blood Stains, Small blood spots on sheets from crushed bugs after feeding.

: Fecal Spots, Dark brown or black spots (bedbug droppings) on bedding and furniture.

: Eggs & Shells, Tiny white eggs and shed skins in mattress seams, bed frames, and furniture cracks.


: Musty Odour, A sweet, musty smell in heavily infested areas due to pheromones released by the bugs.

Watch a bedbug feed


Life Cycle & Reproduction:

: A female bedbug lays 200 to 500 eggs in her lifetime.

: Eggs hatch in 6-10 days, and nymphs mature within 5 weeks.

: Bedbugs can survive months without feeding, making them difficult to eradicate.

Cockroaches in the UK


Cockroaches are resilient pests found in homes, restaurants, and businesses across the UK, particularly in warm and humid environments. While they are not as common as in tropical climates, they can still be a major problem in cities and commercial kitchens.

Common UK Cockroach Species

German Cockroach (Blattella germanica)

: Small (10-15mm long), light brown with two dark stripes on its back.

: Prefers warm, humid environments like kitchens and bathrooms.

: Excellent climbers and often found in cupboards, cracks, and under appliances.

Oriental Cockroach (Blatta orientalis)

: Larger (20-25mm long), dark brown or black with a glossy body.

: Slower-moving and prefers damp, cooler areas like basements, drains, and under floorboards.

: Less likely to climb smooth surfaces compared to the German cockroach.

American Cockroach (Periplaneta americana)

: The largest species in the UK (up to 35mm long), reddish-brown with a yellow band behind its head.

: Rare in homes but found in commercial buildings, sewers, and boiler rooms.

: Can fly short distances.

Where Are Cockroaches Found?

: Kitchens, Behind fridges, under sinks, and inside cupboards.

: Bathrooms, Around pipes, drains, and under tiles.

: Restaurants & Food Establishments, Warm areas with easy access to food and water.

: Sewers & Basements, Especially for the Oriental and American cockroaches.

Signs of a Cockroach Infestation

: Sightings, Seeing cockroaches, especially at night, is a key sign.

: Droppings, Small black droppings (similar to coffee grounds) found along walls or in cupboards.

: Egg Cases (Oothecae), Capsule-like egg cases hidden in cracks or dark places.

: Musty Odour, A strong, unpleasant smell in heavily infested areas.

: Shed Skins, Cockroaches molt multiple times before adulthood, leaving behind

Health Risks & Dangers

: Spread of bacteria, Cockroaches carry harmful bacteria like Salmonella and E. coli, contaminating food and surfaces.

: Trigger allergies & asthma, Their shed skin and droppings can worsen respiratory conditions.

: Food contamination, They can crawl through dirty areas before reaching food storage spaces.

Fleas

Fleas are external parasites, living off the blood of mammals and birds, and include cat/dog fleas, human fleas and rat fleas. Beside the problems posed by the flea itself, they can also act as a vector for disease, for example, they can transmit a variety of viral and bacterial diseases to humans and other animals. Cat/dog fleas are not thought to transmit any serious illness to humans but they can cause severe irritation.


Fleas in the UK Fleas are small, wingless, parasitic insects that feed on the blood of animals and humans. They are a common problem in UK homes, especially for pet owners, as they can quickly infest carpets, furniture, and bedding. Common UK Flea Species 1. Cat Flea (Ctenocephalides felis) The most common flea in the UK. Prefers cats but will also bite dogs and humans. Reddish-brown, about 1–3mm long, with powerful hind legs for jumping. 2. Dog Flea (Ctenocephalides canis) Similar to cat fleas but more commonly found on dogs. Less common than cat fleas in the UK. 3. Human Flea (Pulex irritans) Rare in modern homes but still found in some rural areas. Can infest humans, pigs, and other animals. 4. Bird Fleas Found in bird nests and can enter homes when birds leave the nest. Where Are Fleas Found? On Pets – Fleas live on dogs and cats, especially around the neck, back, and tail. Carpets & Rugs – Flea eggs and larvae develop in soft furnishings. Furniture & Bedding – Fleas hide in sofas, pet beds, and blankets. Garden & Outdoor Areas – Fleas can survive in shaded areas where pets rest. Flea Life Cycle Eggs – Laid on the host but often fall into carpets and bedding. Larvae – Tiny worm-like larvae feed on organic debris and flea droppings. Pupae – Develop inside cocoons and can remain dormant for months. Adults – Fully developed fleas emerge when they sense warmth and movement. Signs of a Flea Infestation 1. Pets Scratching – Excessive scratching, biting, or grooming. 2. Flea Dirt – Small black specks (flea droppings) on pet fur or bedding. 3. Flea Bites – Small red, itchy bites, often on ankles, legs, or waist. 4. Seeing Fleas – Tiny, fast-moving insects in fur, carpets, or furniture. Health Risks of Fleas Skin irritation & allergies – Bites cause itching and rashes. Anemia in pets – Severe flea infestations can lead to blood loss in animals. Tapeworm transmission – Fleas can carry tapeworm larvae, which can infect pets and humans. How to Get Rid of Fleas Treat Pets – Use vet-approved flea treatments like spot-on treatments, flea collars, and oral medications. Vacuum Regularly – Clean carpets, furniture, and pet bedding frequently. Wash Bedding & Soft Furnishings – Wash pet beds, cushions, and blankets at 60°C to kill fleas. Use Flea Sprays & Powders – Apply insecticides to carpets, skirting boards, and pet areas. Wasp


Wasp nests are normally built in sheltered spots, with easy access to outside. They can be found in areas such as wall cavities, roof spaces and under eaves. They can grow to around the size of a football as the summer progresses. Only female wasps sting, but they can do so repeatedly. Honeybees live in colonies often greater than 30,000 in roof and wall cavities, and hollow trees. They swarm in early summer. They have a barbed sting and die once this is used but will sting when provoked. Untreated infestations can cause contamination of food, harm to mortar and building fabric, and threats of stings. Some people may experience anaphylactic shock on being stung and will require prompt treatment.

Flies

Whether they are blue bottle or fruit flies, these pests carry a wide variety of diseases such as food poisoning and dysentery.


Flies in the UK Flies are common insects in the UK, with various species found in homes, businesses, and outdoor spaces. While some are simply a nuisance, others can spread diseases by contaminating food and surfaces. Common Fly Species in the UK 1. House Fly (Musca domestica) The most common fly found in homes and kitchens. About 6–7mm long, grey body, large red eyes, and transparent wings. Feeds on food waste and organic matter. Can spread bacteria like Salmonella and E. coli. 2. Bluebottle Fly (Calliphora vomitoria) Larger than house flies (10–14mm), with a metallic blue body. Attracted to rotting food, waste, and dead animals. Common in bins, compost heaps, and near carcasses. 3. Greenbottle Fly (Lucilia sericata) Similar to bluebottles but with a shiny green body. Often found around decaying organic material and waste. Used in forensic investigations and medical maggot therapy. 4. Fruit Fly (Drosophila melanogaster) Small (2–4mm), yellowish-brown body with red eyes. Attracted to ripe or fermenting fruit, sugary liquids, and alcohol. Breeds rapidly in kitchens and food storage areas. 5. Drain Fly (Psychodidae) (aka Moth Fly) Tiny (1.5–5mm), fuzzy flies with moth-like wings. Found in damp areas, sinks, and drains. Lay eggs in organic sludge inside pipes. 6. Cluster Fly (Pollenia spp.) 8–10mm long, dark grey body with golden hairs. Often found in large groups indoors during autumn and winter. Hibernate in attics, wall cavities, and window frames. 7. Horse Fly (Tabanidae) Large (10–25mm), dark-bodied with large eyes. Females bite humans and animals to feed on blood. Found in rural areas near water and livestock. Where Flies Are Found Kitchens & Homes – Attracted to food, waste, and sugary substances. Bins & Compost Heaps – Ideal breeding grounds for bluebottles and house flies. Drains & Bathrooms – Drain flies thrive in damp environments. Livestock & Farms – Horse flies are common near animals. Health Risks of Flies Disease transmission – Can spread bacteria like E. coli, Salmonella, and Listeria. Food contamination – Flies regurgitate and defecate on food. Painful bites – Horse flies have painful bites that can cause swelling. How to Get Rid of Flies Keep food covered – Store food in sealed containers. Dispose of waste properly – Empty bins regularly and keep them sealed. Clean drains – Remove organic buildup to prevent drain flies. Use fly screens – Fit mesh screens on windows and doors. Fly traps & repellents – Use UV light traps, sticky fly paper, or natural deterrents like vinegar. Professional pest control – For severe infestations, expert treatments may be needed.
